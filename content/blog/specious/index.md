## Meaning

**Specious** means something that appears to be true, correct, or attractive, but is actually false, misleading, or deceptive.
It describes arguments, reasoning, or appearances that seem convincing on the surface but lack real substance or truth.

## Grammar and Usage

- **Part of speech:** Adjective
- **Usage pattern:** `specious argument`, `specious reasoning`, `specious claim`, etc.
- Typically used to criticize ideas, excuses, or logic that _sound good_ but are _not valid or genuine_.

**Example structure:**

- _a specious argument that hides the truth_
- _Her explanation sounded reasonable, but it was specious._

## Common Phrases

- **specious reasoning** – deceptive or flawed logic that sounds convincing
- **specious claim** – a statement that seems true but is false
- **specious excuse** – a false justification that seems reasonable
- **specious argument** – persuasive but deceptive reasoning

## Collocations

- **make a specious claim**
- **give a specious reason**
- **present a specious argument**
- **appear specious**
- **sound specious**

## Examples

1. The politician gave a **specious argument** to justify the tax increase.
2. His apology sounded sincere, but it was **specious**.
3. Don’t be fooled by **specious reasoning**—the facts tell a different story.
4. The company’s claim of “eco-friendly products” turned out to be **specious**.
5. She dismissed his **specious excuse** for missing the meeting.
6. The plan looks good at first glance, but it’s based on **specious logic**.
7. They tried to defend their actions with **specious justifications**.
8. The lawyer exposed the witness’s **specious testimony**.
9. His confidence gave his **specious claim** an air of credibility.
10. The proposal was **specious**, designed to mislead rather than inform.
